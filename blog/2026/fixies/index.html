Overview

What is the minimum mechanism required to turn pedaling into motion? The fundamental components are:

  • A crank to connect your leg motion to the drivetrain (via the pedals)
  • A front gear (called a chainring) that attaches to the crank
  • A back gear (called a cog) that attaches to the back wheel
  • A chain to connect the two gears
  • Wheels to propel the rider forward

Collectively the parts that make the bike move are called the "drivetrain". The specifics of what exactly goes into a drivetrain vary across different types of bikes, but generally they will have these components.

The Chain

Modern bikes have the rider seated in the middle of the frame, there needs to be a way to deliver the power from the pedals to the rear wheel. When pedaling forward, the teeth in the front gear pull the chain forward, which then pulls on the teeth of the rear cog, which drives the entire bike forward. (Note: these gears are also called "sprockets")

While there are other mechanisms you can use to connect the two gears, like a belt or a shaft, chains are light, cheap, simple to maintain, and flexible (more important when we talk about shifting in a later post).

Technically a bike doesn't need a chain or gears. Penny-farthings had a direct-drive action where the pedals were attached directly to the front wheel.

However, that locks you into a 1/1 gear ratio since one full pedal revolution must equal one full wheel revolution. If you were to do this on a normal bike wheel, you would have to pedal like crazy just to get anywhere. In order to move at a practical pace, the only way to go faster in a direct-drive-system is to increase the wheel size, which is why penny-farthings look like that. Gear ratios are what allow us to manufacture and ride bikes more proportional to the human body.

How the Chain and Gears Interact

Like any other chain, the bike chain is made up of individual links. Where it differs from other chains is that each individual link has a roller. As the name implies, the roller rolls as the gear tooth slots into the chain link. This reduces overall friction in the system compared to if the tooth pushed directly on the chain itself.

Less friction means less energy loss and less mechanical wear on the system.

Note the gear teeth themselves have gentle sloping (rather than a perfectly square tooth) to allow the teeth to slot into the gaps of the chain and engage with the rollers.

At any given point, there are multiple gear teeth engaged in the chain. This means that no one tooth is responsible for bearing the entire force of the chain's tension at once. With the teeth engaged in the chain, pedal force presses the rollers more firmly into the sprocket valleys. This means that, under a healthy system, no amount of human-pedaling force will cause the system to disengage. The only ways the chain can be disengaged from the gear teeth then are by manually lifting it out or if the gears or chain themselves are worn or broken.

Gear Ratios

Gear ratio is the ratio of teeth between the front and back gears. Because the chain has a fixed gap between each link (called the chain pitch), a gear with more teeth must be proportionally larger to accommodate those extra teeth.

One example of a gear ratio might be 48 teeth on the chainring and 17 teeth on the back cog, or 48/17 (2.823).

As we decrease the number of teeth in the back gear and/or increase the number of teeth in the front gear, then one full pedal revolution means the back gear will revolve around more times. For a set number of rotations per minute (called a "cadence"), this means you'll go faster than if you had more teeth in the back gear / fewer teeth in the front.

So why doesn't everyone run the largest practical gear ratio possible? Because larger gear ratios mean more effort must be exerted by the rider to move. Everyone who has ridden a bike has experienced trying to climb a hill on their hardest gears and feeling the resistance increase as they begin the ascent.

Levers of a Bike

To understand this, we need to understand how torque works. Torque is the rotational equivalent of force. Torque is equal to the length of the lever multiplied by the force applied. When you tighten a bolt with a wrench, the wrench is the lever. That's why it's easier to tighten a bolt with a longer wrench than a shorter one: Assuming you're applying the same amount of linear force, a longer lever allows you to apply more torque to tighten the bolt.

With bicycles, the levers are the crank arms that attach the front chainring to the pedals. The rider applies a force onto the pedals with his feet and generates torque at the chainring (τcrank). Now the front chainring wants to spin.

Now note that the chainring itself is also a lever. Its lever length is the radius of the chainring from the axle to its teeth (more accurately: its "pitch radius"). As the chainring spins, its teeth pull on the rollers of the chain. This pulling action creates tension in the chain. The chain itself carries this tension, which is a linear force, to the rear cog.

The relationship between chain tension and crank torque is the same as the relationship between force and torque for any other lever:

τcrank = Tchain · rchainring
Tchain = τcrank / rchainring

Now because the chain is also connected to the back teeth, it pulls on the back cogs through the top of the chain as well. Like the chainring, the back cog is also a lever arm; it has a length of rcog. Then, the torque applied to the back wheel is:

τwheel = Tchain · rcog

Substituting our value for Tchain:

τwheel = (τcrank / rchainring) · rcog
τwheel = τcrank · (rcog / rchainring)

Because a smaller rear gear (or larger front gear) produces less wheel torque for the same pedal force, the rider must push harder on the pedals to achieve the same wheel torque.

Now we have τwheel which is twisting on the rear wheel. We have one last conversion to translate this into forward movement for the rider. Like with the sprockets, we can view the wheel itself as a lever with length rwheel.

τwheel spins the wheel which pushes back on the road which in turn pushes forward on the wheel. At the specific patch where the wheel makes contact with the road:

τwheel = Fdrive · rwheel
Fdrive = τwheel / rwheel

Where Fdrive is the force that propels the bike forward.

Putting this all together:

Fdrive = τcrank · (rcog / rchainring) / rwheel
Fdrive = τcrank / (gear ratio · rwheel)

From this we see that increasing speed by increasing the wheel radius or gear ratio comes at a cost. As these values get bigger, Fdrive decreases for the same amount of effort the rider puts in. To maintain the same Fdrive , the rider must apply more force into the pedals.

Chain Tension

Because chain tension is what ultimately delivers power throughout the drivetrain, it's important that the chain stays under tension to ensure it does not fall off the gears.

At the top, the chain is delivering power by pulling on the rear cog, creating torque on the back wheel, so it's here that the chain becomes highly tensioned. At the bottom there is nothing driving the chain as hard, so it becomes less tensioned here.

The important thing to take away here is that tension is not distributed evenly across the entire chain. In fact it is this difference in tension that allows the wheel to rotate; if both sides were evenly tensioned, then the net torque would be zero.

The front teeth inject force into the chain, increasing tension. The rear teeth extract force from the chain to propel the bike, which decreases tension.

Putting it all together: The Fixie

With a fixed-gear bike, or "fixie", the two gears are connected directly by the chain, which means pedaling backwards moves the bike backwards. This also means that your feet are always moving in the direction the bike is moving.

For fixies without dedicated handbrakes, braking is done by resisting the forward momentum of the pedals.

Aside: Fixie Braking

Common gear ratios for fixies are:

  • 46/16 (2.875)
  • 47/17 (2.764)
  • 49/17 (2.882)
  • 48/16 (3)

For fixies without handbrakes, riders need to consider "skid spots", which is a function of their gear ratios. Let's assume a fixie rider has a 48/16 gear ratio and always initiates braking with his right foot at 3 o'clock and left foot at 9 o'clock.

Because gcd(48,16) = 16, the back wheel rotates a perfect integer number of rotations per pedal cycle (in this case, 3). This means that every time the rider brakes, the back wheel will skid in the same position every time.

For a ratio like 48/17, where the number of back teeth do not divide cleanly into the number of front teeth i.e. gcd(48, 17) = 1, the rider will have 17 distinct skid spots, which means less wear on a single spot on the rear tire.

Beyond Fixies

One appeal of fixies is that they're incredibly simple, and simpler mechanisms tend to have fewer spots that can break down. However, the primary downside is that it requires a completely rigid connection between your legs and the pedals.

How can we break that connection while still transmitting power? The answer is a freewheel or freehub, which I'll expand on in the next article of the series.
